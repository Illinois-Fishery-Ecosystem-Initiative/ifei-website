Methodology

The Science

What we measure, why it matters, and how it connects to the health of Northern Illinois lakes.

Note

The metrics below reflect our current technical direction. Final sensor selection will be determined in partnership with the organizations managing these lakes — we build what's actually useful, not what we assume is needed.

01

Water Conductivity

Current target metric

Conductivity measures how well water conducts electricity — a direct function of how many dissolved ions are present. Every salt, mineral, and pollutant that enters a lake changes its conductivity signature.

What conductivity predicts

  • Road salt runoff events — conductivity spikes sharply within hours of salt reaching the lake, a major and underdocumented issue in Northern Illinois winters
  • Storm runoff intensity — sudden drops indicate large freshwater influx, spikes indicate nutrient and ion loading
  • Invasive species risk — zebra mussels cannot establish below 150 µS/cm, making conductivity a direct early warning metric
  • Algal bloom conditions — elevated ions fuel cyanobacteria growth
  • Long-term urbanization impact — conductivity rises steadily as impervious surfaces increase in a watershed

The cascade nobody talks about

Road salt raises conductivity → Daphnia populations crash → algae blooms explode unchecked → oxygen crashes overnight → fish kills → ecosystem collapse. That full sequence is detectable and predictable from continuous conductivity data alone.

Key threshold
150 µS/cm

Minimum conductivity required for zebra mussel establishment. Lakes above this threshold are at active invasion risk.

02

Turbidity

Current target metric

Turbidity measures water clarity by quantifying how much light scatters through suspended particles. Sediment, algae, bacteria, and pollution all increase turbidity.

What turbidity reveals

  • Sediment disturbance from storms, boat traffic, or shoreline erosion
  • Algal bloom onset and progression — turbidity drops as a bloom starts (cyanobacteria are transparent), then spikes as the bloom peaks and dies
  • Zebra mussel presence — they filter water so efficiently that infested lakes become unnaturally clear
  • Post-storm recovery rates

Combined with conductivity

The two metrics together fingerprint event types that neither reveals alone. High conductivity with no turbidity change suggests road salt. High conductivity with high turbidity suggests fertilizer runoff. High conductivity with dropping turbidity suggests zebra mussel activity.

Key insight
Two metrics, one story

Conductivity tells you what's dissolved in the water. Turbidity tells you what's suspended in it. Together they tell you what's happening.

Why Continuous Data

Traditional monitoring

Quarterly manual sampling. A technician visits the lake four times a year and collects a single data point. Everything that happens between visits — storm events, overnight oxygen crashes, seasonal transitions — goes completely unrecorded.

→
IFEI monitoring

A reading every 15–30 minutes, 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. A conductivity spike at 3am after a rainstorm. A turbidity drop over three days as a bloom clears. A seasonal conductivity rise as road salt accumulates through winter. All captured automatically.

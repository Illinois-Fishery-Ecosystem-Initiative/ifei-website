Who We Are

About IFEI

Two high school students from Buffalo Grove, Illinois — building the monitoring infrastructure our local lakes need.

The Origin

We've been fishing Northern Illinois lakes for as long as we can remember. These aren't abstract ecosystems to us — they're places we know by name, by season, by the fish that live in them.

Over time, we started watching spots close. Harmful algal blooms shutting down lakes we'd fished for years. The water turning green and toxic, signs going up, and then nothing — no explanation of what caused it, no timeline for when it might be safe again.

What struck us wasn't just the closures. It was how predictable they felt in hindsight, and how unprepared everyone seemed. Park districts finding out about blooms when someone called to complain. No continuous data. No early warning. Just a quarterly water sample and hope.

That experience pushed us to build something practical — low-cost sensors that give the people managing these lakes the continuous data they need to act before conditions become a crisis.

"We've watched spots we love become inaccessible. That experience is what pushed us to start building something practical."

Mission

To generate open, continuous water quality data for Northern Illinois lakes — in partnership with the organizations already working to protect them.

Our Approach

01

Community first

Before finalizing what our devices measure, we ask the people managing these lakes what data would actually be useful to them. Technology built without that conversation solves the wrong problem.

02

Radically low cost

Each unit targets a sub-$50 bill of materials. If a sensor network requires grant funding to deploy, it won't scale. Ours should be replicable by any school, watershed group, or park district in the country.

03

Open by default

All data, firmware, and hardware designs are published openly on GitHub. We build for the commons. Any community can fork our methodology and deploy their own network.

04

Continuous, not periodic

A quarterly water sample misses everything that happens between visits. Our sensors log every 15–30 minutes, 24 hours a day — capturing the storm events, overnight crashes, and seasonal shifts that quarterly sampling never sees.
